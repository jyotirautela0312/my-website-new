MENU Retro Walk 2019 WRITE

Many young adults struggle to interpret and act on their emotional states, even as mood-tracking tools generate abundant but opaque data. Existing systems rarely translate signals into clear, situated guidance, leaving users unsure how to respond.

Objective The Project
Design
Prototype

This project explores how ideas from affective computing and HCI can help young adults make sense of their moods in everyday activities. I draw on models that map emotions into a smaller set of states (for example, along valence–arousal axes or simple clusters like calm, tense, focused). The aim is to design an activity-focused system that turns these clustered emotional states into clear, practical prompts, gently supporting mood regulation and day-to-day productivity.

Understanding the scope of the brief by opening up the topic

How do young adults actually experience their moods in the middle of everyday life? Can a system move beyond raw mood scores to help them notice, name, and act on what they feel?

Research questions ➜
FUNCTIONAL USE CASES FOR AUTONOMOUS MOOD REGULATION
FROM MOOD SENSING → SELF-REGULATION
A

Triggers & overload

Everyday frictions at home, work and online that slowly build emotional load for young adults.

Sources of affective load
◎

Sensory & workload overload

How might we surface a tiny check-in when deadlines, noise or crowded spaces start to build up, before users reach breaking point?

☻

Social friction & comparison

How might we notice when social media, family expectations or peer conflicts amplify shame or self-doubt, and offer grounding instead of more notifications?

⚙

Hidden emotions under “productivity”

How might we recognise when over-working or constant “busy-ness” is masking anxiety or sadness, and invite a slower, kinder option?

!

Risky coping & withdrawal

How might we detect withdrawal or numbing patterns and suggest safer micro-interventions without judgement or alarm?

B

In-the-moment regulation

When a trigger hits and emotions peak, Aura Mist offers one clear, doable action instead of heavy options.

Just-in-time support
●

Detecting the episode

How might we combine self-reports, routines and behaviours like hyperactivity or procrastination to sense when an episode is starting?

➜

One clear next step

How might we offer exactly one next step—breathe, walk, write, reach out—so even an overwhelmed user can act?

☁

Multisensory micro-ritual

How might we choreograph a 2–5 minute ritual with scent, a small prompt and a tiny activity so regulation feels embodied, not only “in the head”?

∆

Emotion-family adaptation

How might we adapt the ritual for different clusters such as tense, overloaded or shut-down, instead of treating all “bad mood” the same?

C

Reflection & long-term change

Turning scattered episodes into patterns, language and small habits so users slowly trust themselves with their moods.

Mood literacy & habits
✶

Simple emotion clusters

How might we cluster raw labels into a few relatable families— calm, tense, overloaded, shut-down—so users can name what they feel?

☰

Pattern views, not just graphs

How might we show mood history as patterns like “exam weeks” or “late-night scrolling” instead of only charts and percentages?

✓

Strategy effectiveness

How might we quietly track which strategies actually help each person and surface them back as gentle suggestions?

✉

Sharing & support

How might we create respectful, consent-based summaries that users can share with a friend or therapist when they need more help?

With Aura Mist

Clustered mood states, one clear nudge, and a tiny ritual that fits quietly into the user’s day.

A small ecosystem for autonomous mood regulation.

Aura Mist links mood sensing, multisensory rituals and reflection, so affective signals become gentle, actionable guidance instead of overwhelming data.

Stakeholders

  • Young adults juggling study and work
  • Caregivers and close friends
  • Therapists / counsellors (optional hand-off)

Key components

  • Lightweight mood & body check-ins
  • Scent diffuser / object for micro-rituals
  • Short, clustered emotion scripts
  • Pattern views for reflection

Key proposition

A gentle system that helps users notice, name and regulate moods without feeling clinical, heavy or judged.

Pillar 1 · Personal sensing

Small mood and body prompts woven into daily routines, not extra “homework”.

Pillar 2 · In-the-moment ritual

2–5 minute multisensory routines triggered when clusters like tense, overloaded or shut-down appear.

Pillar 3 · Reflection & sharing

Simple pattern views and optional summaries that can stay private or be shared with a professional.

THE PROCESS

We listed the main use-cases and defined what “success” meant. We chose the sensor layout and parts (chip, battery, IMU) and kept delay and power low. We coded fast sampling with a simple 3-step setup. When posture was wrong, the app showed a 15–30 s tutorial, then the insole checked the pressure pattern and said pass/try again. We bench-tested, compared with a force plate, ran a short home pilot, kept data on the device, and improved to V2.
