MENU Insane Habits — Collaborative Campus Space 2024 WRITE

This project began with a simple question: how do students and faculty actually find space to think together on campus? Rather than design an “innovation hub” app, I treated IIT Roorkee as a living lab and followed the everyday routes, shortcuts and stopovers where collaboration already happens quietly.

The work sits between spatial design and social research. It maps informal study spots, overheard critiques, and chance meetings, then uses those observations to sketch a low-threshold collaborative shell—something people can drift into without needing permission or a booking.

OBJECTIVE THE PROJECT
Fieldwork
Spatial Prototyping

The goal was to understand how collaboration is shaped by corridors, corners and meeting rooms, then design a small intervention that lowers the threshold to join in. Instead of promising more productivity, the project asks how space can make it easier to sit together, share work-in-progress and step away when needed.

RESEARCH APPROACH

I started with simple tools: walking, sketching, and talking. I mapped where people actually sit when they are stuck, excited, or exhausted—stairs, studio thresholds, canteen corners. Short intercept interviews and shadowing sessions revealed patterns: booked rooms that stay empty, noisy lobbies that still attract group work, and a quiet wish for spaces that feel less formal than a seminar room and less exposed than a café.

KEY INSIGHTS

Across departments, people described similar frictions: never-available “formal” rooms, awkward power dynamics when faculty join, and very few neutral spaces where it feels safe to bump into another discipline. Many collaborations start as passing comments in the wrong places and simply die there.

What people asked for was not another platform, but a physical anchor for cross-talk: somewhere you can sit alone, join a conversation, or quietly listen without feeling you are intruding.

THE INTERVENTION

A low-threshold collaborative shell that sits between corridor and room.

The proposal layers a warm inner volume for critiques and quiet work with a more porous outer skin that leaks sound, light and views back to the campus path. Seating allows five-minute drop-ins as well as longer sessions. The architecture is tuned for partial attention: you can notice something happening, linger at the edge, then decide if you want to step in.
