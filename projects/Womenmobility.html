MENU LOW-FLOOR MOBILITY AT HOME 2025 THESIS PDF

Co-Speculating Safer Movement in Domestic Context for People with Lower-Limb Disabilities.

In many Indian homes, most daily work still happens on or near the floor: cooking, bathing, praying, caring for others. Mobility aids, however, are built for hospital corridors and majorly for public infrastructure and mostly for standing bodies. This project listens to how individual with mobility impairment actually move through these low-floor routines, where pain and risk show up, and how they imagine safer ways of living in the same homes.

Literature & existing tools

What research already knows about low-floor mobility

Gaps I chose to work with
  • Very little work follows women across rooms as they move between cot, floor, toilet and bathing areas.
  • Domestic labour postures (cooking, washing, childcare) are discussed, but rarely linked to falls and long-term mobility loss.
  • Assistive products are treated as objects, not as things that must fit tight layouts, shared homes and family negotiations.
Fieldwork in homes

What low-floor mobility looks like in everyday rooms

I visited homes in Delhi and Uttarakhand, sitting on the floor with women and families to see how cooking, bathing, prayer and rest actually happen when legs don’t fully cooperate.

Photos are anonymised and shared with consent; layouts and details are simplified to protect privacy.

Body, room and floor

Mapping how bodies meet domestic surfaces

I drew simple maps of homes and bodies together: how a woman moves from cot to floor, from stove to bathroom, and where bending, kneeling or twisting quietly accumulates strain.
