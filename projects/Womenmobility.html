MENU LOW-FLOOR MOBILITY AT HOME 2025 THESIS PDF

Co-Speculating Safer Movement in Domestic Context for People with Lower-Limb Disabilities.

In many Indian homes, most daily work still happens on or near the floor: cooking, bathing, praying, caring for others. Mobility aids, however, are built for hospital corridors and majorly for public infrastructure and mostly for standing bodies. This project listens to how individual with mobility impairment actually move through these low-floor routines, where pain and risk show up, and how they imagine safer ways of living in the same homes.

Method

Multi-phase, participatory inquiry (Phase 0–2)

We conducted three sequential phases (Phase 0–2) to examine low-floor mobility in domestic contexts, combining semi-structured interviews with two bespoke booklet-based workshops. We asked participants to document everyday movement between floor, cot, kitchen, toilet, and bathing spaces, and we gathered filled booklets, sketches, photographs, video recordings, and written reflections. All activities were conducted with IEC approval and informed consent.

Phase 0

Foundation

We reviewed prior work and existing mobility aids to map what “low-floor” living demands.

We gathered early signals from field visits to identify where clinical assumptions break inside domestic routines.

Phase 1

In-home inquiry

We conducted contextual walkthroughs and semi-structured interviews across rooms (cot–floor–kitchen–toilet–bath).

We recorded video (with consent) to capture transfers, thresholds, and posture sequences that are hard to report verbally.

Phase 2

Co-speculation

We asked participants to annotate and reshape prompts (booklet + artefacts) to surface “needs vs wants” in assistive support.

We coded transcripts and artefacts into emergent themes, then translated them into design directions for Phases 3–4.

Phase 0 Semi-structured interviews 30 min · Oct 2025 · CHC Dasna

We conducted semi-structured interviews with adults with lower-limb disabilities during routine clinical visits. Conversations focused on daily domestic activities, mobility challenges, and lived experiences of using assistive devices.

  • Participants: 8 (3 women, 5 men)
  • Data: discussion notes, written responses, household photographs
  • Media: photographs and videos (with consent)
  • Ethics: IEC approval obtained; voluntary participation
Phase 1 Bespoke booklet workshop I 20 min · Nov 2025 · ISIC, New Delhi

We facilitated a guided booklet workshop using visual prompts and speculative stimuli to elicit imaginaries of domestic mobility. Participants responded through sketches, annotations, stickers, and open discussion.

  • Participants: 14 (3 women, 11 men)
  • Inputs: fictional household images, current assistive devices
  • Outputs: filled booklets, sketches, written notes
  • Goal: surface tacit needs and imaginative alternatives
Phase 2 Clinical reflection 20 min · Nov 2025 · ISIC · ALIMCO · CHC NCR

We gathered reflections from clinicians and rehabilitation professionals on participant responses. These discussions contextualised speculative ideas within clinical feasibility, risk, and long-term use.

  • Participants: 3 clinicians + 1 nodal officer
  • Data: concept notes, expert reflections
  • Analysis: emergent thematic synthesis
  • Forward link: informs Phases 3–4 (ongoing)
Analysis

Emergent themes across phases

Movement as negotiation

Participants described mobility not as a single action, but as a continuous negotiation between floor, furniture, walls, and caregivers.

Normalization of strain

Postures that caused pain or long-term harm were often described as “normal” or unavoidable within domestic routines.

Acceptance over optimization

Ease of use, storage, and social acceptability mattered more than biomechanical efficiency when adopting low-floor supports.

What came next

How these themes informed phases 3–4

The first three phases surfaced recurring patterns in low-floor domestic mobility. These insights shaped the prompts, speculative mechanisms, and feedback loops planned for the next stages.

  • Movement as negotiation → Phase 3 uses co-speculative prompts to redesign “checkpoints” (thresholds, transfers, floor–cot transitions) as shared decision points.
  • Normalization of strain → Phase 3 makes “invisible pain” discussable through comparative scenarios (current vs imagined supports) and body–room mapping.
  • Acceptance over optimization → Phase 4 tests AI-generated concept variations against acceptability, upkeep, and domestic fit—rather than only performance.

This project is actively developing a participatory framework to address many of the areas of opportunity
revealed through interviews, clinical reflections, and co-speculative workshops on low-floor domestic mobility.
